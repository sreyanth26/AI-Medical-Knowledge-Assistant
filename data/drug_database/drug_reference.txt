DRUG 1: PARACETAMOL (ACETAMINOPHEN)
========================================

Generic Name: Paracetamol
Brand Names: Calpol, Tylenol, Panadol, Dolo 650
Drug Class: Analgesic / Antipyretic (non-opioid)
Mechanism of Action:
Inhibits prostaglandin synthesis in the central nervous system (CNS).
Acts on the hypothalamic heat-regulating center to reduce fever.
Does NOT have significant anti-inflammatory activity (unlike NSAIDs).

INDICATIONS
- Mild to moderate pain: headache, toothache, musculoskeletal pain, dysmenorrhea
- Post-operative pain (mild)
- Fever reduction in adults and children
- Osteoarthritis pain management

DOSAGE
Adults:
- Standard: 500mg to 1000mg (1g) every 4–6 hours as needed
- Maximum: 4000mg (4g) per 24-hour period
- Reduce to 2g/day in: elderly, hepatic impairment, chronic alcohol users, malnourished

Children (by weight):
- 10–15 mg/kg body weight every 4–6 hours
- Maximum 5 doses in 24 hours
- Maximum 75 mg/kg/day

Elderly: Start with lower doses (500mg every 6 hours); reduce max to 3g/day

Renal impairment: Extend interval to every 6–8 hours

CONTRAINDICATIONS
- Known hypersensitivity to paracetamol
- Severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh C)
- Hypersensitivity to any excipients

ADVERSE EFFECTS
Rare at therapeutic doses. Main concern is OVERDOSE.
- Hepatotoxicity (liver failure) — most serious, from overdose
- Skin reactions: rare rashes, urticaria
- Blood disorders: thrombocytopenia (very rare)
- Hypersensitivity reactions (rare)

OVERDOSE
Signs appear 24–72 hours after ingestion:
- Early (0–24h): Nausea, vomiting, malaise (may appear normal)
- Late (24–72h): Right upper quadrant pain, jaundice, liver failure
ANTIDOTE: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — most effective within 8 hours of ingestion
Treatment: Activated charcoal if within 1 hour; IV NAC protocol

DRUG INTERACTIONS
- Warfarin: Enhanced anticoagulant effect (monitor INR with regular use)
- Alcohol (chronic use): Significantly increased hepatotoxicity risk
- Carbamazepine, Rifampicin, Phenytoin: Increased metabolism of paracetamol (reduced efficacy and increased toxic metabolite)
- Isoniazid: Increased hepatotoxicity risk

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
- Safe in pregnancy (all trimesters) at recommended doses
- Safe during breastfeeding
- Preferred analgesic/antipyretic for dengue fever (avoid NSAIDs)
- Available OTC; educate patients on maximum daily dose

DRUG 2: AMOXICILLIN
========================================

Generic Name: Amoxicillin
Brand Names: Amoxil, Trimox, Moxatag
Drug Class: Aminopenicillin (Beta-lactam antibiotic)
Mechanism of Action:
Bactericidal. Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to
Penicillin-Binding Proteins (PBPs), causing cell lysis and death.

SPECTRUM OF ACTIVITY
Gram-positive: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Streptococcus pyogenes, Enterococcus faecalis
Gram-negative: Haemophilus influenzae, E. coli, Proteus mirabilis
Also: Helicobacter pylori (in combination therapy)
NOT effective against: MRSA, beta-lactamase-producing organisms (use Amoxicillin-clavulanate)

INDICATIONS
- Upper respiratory tract infections: otitis media, sinusitis, tonsillitis
- Lower respiratory tract infections: community-acquired pneumonia
- Urinary tract infections (UTI) — uncomplicated
- Skin and soft tissue infections
- Dental infections and prophylaxis
- Helicobacter pylori eradication (combination regimen)
- Lyme disease (early stage)

DOSAGE
Adults:
- Mild-moderate infections: 250–500mg every 8 hours OR 500–875mg every 12 hours
- Severe infections: 875mg every 12 hours or 1g every 8 hours
- Pneumonia: 500mg three times daily or 1g twice daily
- H. pylori eradication: 1g twice daily (with clarithromycin 500mg BD + PPI BD for 7–14 days)
- Dental prophylaxis: 2g single dose 30–60 minutes before procedure

Children:
- 25–50 mg/kg/day divided every 8–12 hours
- Severe infections: 80–90 mg/kg/day

Duration: 5–7 days (most infections); 7–10 days (strep throat to prevent rheumatic fever)

CONTRAINDICATIONS
- Hypersensitivity to penicillins (history of anaphylaxis — absolute contraindication)
- History of amoxicillin-associated jaundice or hepatic dysfunction
- Infectious mononucleosis (Epstein-Barr virus) — causes extensive maculopapular rash

ADVERSE EFFECTS
Common:
- Diarrhea (up to 10%)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Skin rash (maculopapular rash — especially in mononucleosis)

Serious (rare):
- Anaphylaxis (0.01–0.05% of penicillin-treated patients)
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome (very rare)
- Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea (pseudomembranous colitis)
- Seizures (at very high doses, especially in renal failure)
- Hepatotoxicity (rare)

DRUG INTERACTIONS
- Warfarin: May enhance anticoagulant effect (monitor INR)
- Oral contraceptives: Theoretical reduction in efficacy (advise additional contraception)
- Allopurinol: Increased incidence of skin rashes
- Probenecid: Reduces renal excretion of amoxicillin (increases levels — may be intentional)
- Methotrexate: Amoxicillin reduces methotrexate clearance (toxicity risk)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
- Cross-reactivity with cephalosporins: ~1–2% in penicillin-allergic patients
- Renally cleared: reduce dose in CrCl <30 mL/min
- Safe in pregnancy (Category B)
- Reconstituted oral suspension: refrigerate and use within 14 days

DRUG 3: METFORMIN
========================================

Generic Name: Metformin Hydrochloride
Brand Names: Glucophage, Glumetza, Fortamet
Drug Class: Biguanide (Antidiabetic)
Mechanism of Action:
- Decreases hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis) — primary mechanism
- Increases insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues (muscle, fat)
- Slows intestinal glucose absorption
- Does NOT stimulate insulin secretion (no hypoglycemia risk when used alone)

INDICATIONS
- Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (first-line medication per WHO, ADA, IDF guidelines)
- Prediabetes (to delay progression to type 2 DM)
- Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) — off-label but widely used
- Prevention of type 2 diabetes in high-risk individuals

DOSAGE
Adults:
- Starting dose: 500mg twice daily OR 850mg once daily with meals
- Titration: Increase by 500mg weekly or 850mg every 2 weeks
- Target dose: 2000mg/day in divided doses (most effective)
- Maximum: 2550mg/day (2000mg is often sufficient)
- Extended-release (XR): 500mg–2000mg once daily with evening meal (better GI tolerance)

CONTRAINDICATIONS
- eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m² (risk of lactic acidosis — ABSOLUTE contraindication)
- eGFR 30–45: Use with caution, dose reduction required
- Active or recent contrast dye use: Hold 48 hours before and after iodinated contrast
- Hepatic impairment (significant)
- Alcohol abuse
- Acute or chronic metabolic acidosis
- Dehydration / sepsis / hemodynamic instability

ADVERSE EFFECTS
Common (GI — usually transient, improves with food and dose titration):
- Nausea, vomiting (most common reason for discontinuation)
- Diarrhea
- Abdominal discomfort, metallic taste

Serious (rare):
- Lactic acidosis: Rare but potentially fatal; risk increases with renal impairment
  Signs: muscle pain, weakness, difficulty breathing, abdominal pain, nausea
- Vitamin B12 deficiency: Long-term use reduces B12 absorption (check B12 annually)

DRUG INTERACTIONS
- Alcohol: Increases risk of lactic acidosis
- Iodinated contrast agents: Hold metformin 48h before and after
- Cimetidine: Increases metformin levels (reduces renal clearance)
- Diuretics, ACE inhibitors: May affect renal function and metformin clearance
- Topiramate, Acetazolamide: Increased lactic acidosis risk

MONITORING
- HbA1c: Every 3 months until stable, then every 6 months
- Renal function (eGFR/creatinine): Before starting, at 3–6 months, then annually
- Vitamin B12: Annual check in long-term users
- Liver function: Baseline and periodically

DRUG 4: AMLODIPINE
========================================

Generic Name: Amlodipine Besylate
Brand Names: Norvasc, Amlo, Stamlo
Drug Class: Calcium Channel Blocker (Dihydropyridine)
Mechanism of Action:
Blocks voltage-gated L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle and cardiac muscle.
Results in: vasodilation (reduces peripheral resistance and blood pressure)
            reduced myocardial oxygen demand (antianginal effect)

INDICATIONS
- Hypertension (first-line agent, especially in elderly and Black patients)
- Stable angina pectoris
- Vasospastic angina (Prinzmetal's angina)

DOSAGE
Adults:
- Hypertension: Start 5mg once daily; may increase to 10mg once daily after 7–14 days
- Angina: 5–10mg once daily
- Elderly / hepatic impairment: Start with 2.5mg once daily

CONTRAINDICATIONS
- Hypersensitivity to amlodipine or other dihydropyridines
- Severe hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg)
- Cardiogenic shock
- Unstable angina (severe, use with caution)

ADVERSE EFFECTS
Common:
- Peripheral edema (ankle swelling) — most common, dose-dependent
- Flushing, headache, dizziness
- Fatigue, palpitations

Serious (rare):
- Severe hypotension
- Myocardial infarction (rare, worsening angina on initiation)
- Hepatotoxicity (rare)

DRUG INTERACTIONS
- CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin, ketoconazole, grapefruit juice): Increase amlodipine levels
- CYP3A4 inducers (rifampicin, carbamazepine): Reduce amlodipine levels
- Simvastatin: Amlodipine increases simvastatin exposure (risk of myopathy — limit simvastatin to 20mg)
- Cyclosporine/Tacrolimus: Amlodipine increases levels

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
- Long half-life (~35–50 hours): Once-daily dosing; missed doses less critical
- Safe in renal impairment (not renally eliminated)
- Can be used in pregnancy-induced hypertension (second/third trimester)
- Does NOT affect heart rate (unlike beta-blockers or non-dihydropyridine CCBs)

DRUG 5: INSULIN (Overview)
========================================

Types of Insulin:

RAPID-ACTING:
- Examples: Insulin Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog), Glulisine (Apidra)
- Onset: 10–30 min | Peak: 30 min–3 hrs | Duration: 3–5 hrs
- Use: Mealtime insulin (inject just before or with meals)

SHORT-ACTING (Regular):
- Examples: Humulin R, Novolin R
- Onset: 30–60 min | Peak: 2–4 hrs | Duration: 5–8 hrs
- Use: IV infusion in ICU, or SC 30 min before meals

INTERMEDIATE-ACTING:
- Examples: NPH Insulin (Humulin N, Novolin N)
- Onset: 1–3 hrs | Peak: 4–12 hrs | Duration: 12–18 hrs
- Use: Basal insulin, twice-daily dosing

LONG-ACTING (Basal):
- Examples: Glargine (Lantus, Basaglar), Detemir (Levemir), Degludec (Tresiba)
- Onset: 1–4 hrs | Peak: Peakless (flat profile) | Duration: 20–42 hrs
- Use: Once daily basal dosing; does not cause peak hypoglycemia

PREMIXED:
- Examples: 70/30 (70% NPH + 30% Regular), BiAsp 30
- Use: Twice daily with meals; convenient but less flexible

INSULIN STORAGE
- Unopened: Refrigerate at 2–8°C (do not freeze)
- In use (opened): Room temperature (up to 25°C) for up to 28–30 days

HYPOGLYCEMIA (Insulin Side Effect)
Symptoms: Sweating, trembling, palpitations, confusion, loss of consciousness
Treatment (conscious patient): 15–20g fast-acting carbohydrates (glucose tablets, juice, sugar)
Treatment (unconscious): 1mg Glucagon IM/SC, or IV Dextrose 50% (25mL)
