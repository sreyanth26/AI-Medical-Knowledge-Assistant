DENGUE FEVER — CLINICAL GUIDELINES (Sample)
Source: WHO Model Treatment Guidelines (Illustrative Sample)

1. OVERVIEW
Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus (DENV),
transmitted by Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes. There are four distinct
serotypes (DENV-1 to DENV-4). It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions.

2. CLINICAL FEATURES
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C)
- Severe headache
- Pain behind the eyes (retro-orbital pain)
- Muscle and joint pain (breakbone fever)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Skin rash appearing 2–5 days after fever onset
- Mild bleeding manifestations (nose or gum bleed, easy bruising)

3. DIAGNOSIS
- NS1 antigen test: positive in first 1–5 days
- IgM/IgG serology: positive from day 5 onward
- Complete blood count (CBC): thrombocytopenia and leukopenia are hallmarks

4. TREATMENT
Dengue fever treatment is primarily supportive. There is NO specific antiviral therapy.

4.1 Fluid Management
- Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) for mild cases
- IV isotonic fluids (normal saline or Ringer's lactate) if patient cannot tolerate orals
- Monitor urine output (target ≥0.5 mL/kg/hour)

4.2 Fever Management
- Paracetamol (acetaminophen): 500mg–1g every 4–6 hours; maximum 4g/day
- DO NOT use aspirin or ibuprofen — increases risk of bleeding and Reye syndrome

4.3 Warning Signs Requiring Hospitalization
- Severe abdominal pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Rapid breathing, fluid accumulation
- Fatigue/restlessness
- Liver enlargement >2 cm
- Platelet count <20,000/mm³

5. PREVENTION
- No specific treatment; prevention focuses on vector control
- Dengvaxia vaccine available in some endemic countries for seropositive individuals ≥9 years old
- Eliminate mosquito breeding sites (standing water)
- Personal protection: repellents, long clothing, bed nets

PARACETAMOL (ACETAMINOPHEN) — DRUG INFORMATION

Generic Name: Paracetamol (INN) / Acetaminophen (USAN)
Drug Class: Analgesic / Antipyretic
Mechanism: Inhibits prostaglandin synthesis in the CNS; acts on hypothalamic heat-regulation center

INDICATIONS
- Mild to moderate pain (headache, musculoskeletal pain, dysmenorrhea)
- Fever reduction

DOSAGE — ADULTS
- Standard dose: 500 mg to 1 g every 4–6 hours as needed
- Maximum daily dose: 4 g (4000 mg) in 24 hours
- Reduce to maximum 2 g/day in patients with hepatic impairment or chronic alcohol use

DOSAGE — CHILDREN
- 10–15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours
- Maximum 5 doses in 24 hours
- Do not exceed 75 mg/kg/day

CONTRAINDICATIONS
- Known hypersensitivity to paracetamol
- Severe hepatic impairment

SIDE EFFECTS
- Rare at therapeutic doses
- Overdose: Hepatotoxicity (liver damage) — this is a medical emergency
- Overdose antidote: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — administer within 8–10 hours

DRUG INTERACTIONS
- Warfarin: may enhance anticoagulant effect
- Alcohol: combined use increases hepatotoxicity risk
- Carbamazepine / rifampicin: may increase metabolism (reduce efficacy)

AMOXICILLIN — DRUG INFORMATION

Generic Name: Amoxicillin
Drug Class: Aminopenicillin (beta-lactam antibiotic)
Mechanism: Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs)

INDICATIONS
- Upper respiratory tract infections (tonsillitis, sinusitis, otitis media)
- Lower respiratory tract infections (community-acquired pneumonia)
- Urinary tract infections (UTI)
- Skin and soft tissue infections
- Helicobacter pylori eradication (in combination regimens)

DOSAGE — ADULTS
- Standard: 250–500 mg every 8 hours, or 500–875 mg every 12 hours
- Severe infections: 1 g every 8 hours
- H. pylori: 1 g twice daily (with clarithromycin + PPI)

DOSAGE — CHILDREN
- 25–50 mg/kg/day divided every 8–12 hours

CONTRAINDICATIONS
- Penicillin allergy
- Infectious mononucleosis (risk of maculopapular rash)

SIDE EFFECTS
- Diarrhea (common)
- Nausea, vomiting
- Skin rash
- Allergic reactions (urticaria, anaphylaxis — rare but serious)

RESISTANCE NOTE
Many common organisms have developed beta-lactamase resistance to amoxicillin alone.
Amoxicillin-clavulanate (co-amoxiclav) is often used to overcome this resistance.

MALARIA — CLINICAL OVERVIEW

Causative Agents: Plasmodium falciparum, P. vivax, P. malariae, P. ovale, P. knowlesi
Transmission: Female Anopheles mosquito bite

SYMPTOMS
Classic malaria triad:
1. Fever (cyclic, every 48–72 hours depending on species)
2. Chills / rigors
3. Sweating

Other symptoms:
- Headache, malaise, myalgia
- Nausea and vomiting
- Splenomegaly (enlarged spleen) in chronic cases
- Severe P. falciparum: cerebral malaria, severe anemia, respiratory distress

DIAGNOSIS
- Peripheral blood smear (gold standard)
- Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) for HRP-2 antigen
- PCR for species confirmation

TREATMENT
First-line uncomplicated P. falciparum:
- Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT)
  e.g., Artemether-lumefantrine (AL) for 3 days

Severe malaria:
- IV Artesunate (preferred over quinine)
- Hospitalization required

P. vivax / P. ovale:
- Chloroquine (where sensitive) + Primaquine to eliminate hypnozoites
- Check G6PD status before primaquine (risk of hemolysis)

PREVENTION
- Insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs)
- Indoor residual spraying (IRS)
- Chemoprophylaxis for travelers: doxycycline, atovaquone-proguanil, or mefloquine
