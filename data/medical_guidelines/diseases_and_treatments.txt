SECTION 1: DENGUE FEVER
========================================

OVERVIEW
Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus (DENV).
Four serotypes exist: DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, DENV-4.
Transmitted by Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes.
Affects tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

SYMPTOMS
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C / 102–104°F)
- Severe headache (frontal)
- Retro-orbital pain (pain behind the eyes)
- Muscle pain (myalgia) and joint pain (arthralgia) — called "breakbone fever"
- Nausea and vomiting
- Skin rash (appears 2–5 days after fever onset)
- Mild bleeding: nosebleed, gum bleed, easy bruising
- Fatigue and weakness

WARNING SIGNS (Severe Dengue — Seek immediate hospital care)
- Severe abdominal pain or tenderness
- Persistent vomiting (3 or more times in 24 hours)
- Rapid breathing or difficulty breathing
- Bleeding from gums or nose
- Blood in urine, stool, or vomit
- Fatigue, restlessness, irritability
- Pale, cold, or clammy skin
- Platelet count below 20,000/mm³

DIAGNOSIS
- NS1 antigen test: positive in days 1–5 of illness
- IgM antibody: positive from day 5 onward (acute infection)
- IgG antibody: positive from day 7 (indicates past or secondary infection)
- Complete Blood Count (CBC): thrombocytopenia (low platelets) and leukopenia (low WBC)

TREATMENT
No specific antiviral drug exists. Treatment is supportive:

Mild Dengue (outpatient):
- Complete bed rest
- Oral rehydration: minimum 2–3 liters of fluids per day (water, ORS, coconut water, juice)
- Paracetamol (acetaminophen): 500mg–1g every 6 hours for fever (maximum 4g/day)
- Do NOT use: Aspirin, Ibuprofen, NSAIDs — increases bleeding risk and platelet dysfunction

Severe Dengue (hospitalization required):
- IV isotonic fluids: Normal saline or Ringer's lactate at 5–10 mL/kg/hour
- Monitor vital signs every hour
- Blood transfusion if hematocrit drops significantly
- Platelet transfusion if <10,000/mm³ with active bleeding

PREVENTION
- Eliminate mosquito breeding sites (standing water in pots, tyres, containers)
- Use insect repellent (DEET-based)
- Wear long-sleeved clothing
- Use mosquito nets and screens
- Dengvaxia vaccine: available for seropositive individuals aged 9–45 years in endemic areas

PROGNOSIS
- Uncomplicated dengue: resolves in 7–10 days with proper supportive care
- Mortality: <1% with good medical management; up to 20% in untreated severe dengue

SECTION 2: MALARIA
========================================

OVERVIEW
Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites.
Species: P. falciparum (most dangerous), P. vivax, P. malariae, P. ovale, P. knowlesi
Transmitted by the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes.

SYMPTOMS
Classic malaria triad:
1. Cold stage: chills, rigors (30–60 minutes)
2. Hot stage: fever (39–41°C), headache, vomiting (2–6 hours)
3. Sweating stage: profuse sweating, temperature drops (2–4 hours)

Cycle: Every 48 hours (P. vivax, P. ovale) or 72 hours (P. malariae)
Other symptoms: headache, myalgia, fatigue, nausea, splenomegaly

Severe P. falciparum Malaria Signs:
- Cerebral malaria: confusion, seizures, coma
- Severe anemia (Hb < 7 g/dL)
- Respiratory distress
- Hypoglycemia
- Acute kidney injury
- Blackwater fever (hemoglobinuria — dark urine)

DIAGNOSIS
- Peripheral blood smear (Giemsa stain): gold standard, identifies species and parasite density
- Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT): detects HRP-2 antigen (P. falciparum specific)
- PCR: most sensitive, used for species confirmation and mixed infections
- CBC: anemia, thrombocytopenia, elevated WBC in severe disease

TREATMENT
Uncomplicated P. falciparum (first-line):
- Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL): 80/480mg twice daily for 3 days (adult dose)
- Artesunate + Amodiaquine: alternative ACT
- Always take with food (fat increases absorption of lumefantrine)

Severe Malaria:
- IV Artesunate: 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24 hours, then daily (preferred over quinine)
- If artesunate unavailable: IV Quinine + Doxycycline
- Hospitalization mandatory
- Monitor blood glucose frequently (hypoglycemia risk)

P. vivax / P. ovale (radical cure):
- Chloroquine: 25 mg/kg total dose over 3 days (if chloroquine-sensitive region)
- Primaquine: 0.25 mg/kg/day for 14 days to eliminate liver hypnozoites
  IMPORTANT: Test for G6PD deficiency before giving primaquine — can cause hemolytic anemia

PREVENTION
- Insecticide-Treated Nets (ITNs)
- Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS)
- Chemoprophylaxis for travelers:
  * Atovaquone-proguanil (Malarone): 1 tablet daily, start 1–2 days before travel
  * Doxycycline: 100mg daily, start 1–2 days before travel
  * Mefloquine: weekly, start 2–3 weeks before travel
- Mosquito repellents containing DEET

SECTION 3: TYPHOID FEVER
========================================

OVERVIEW
Typhoid fever is a systemic bacterial infection caused by Salmonella Typhi.
Transmitted via contaminated food and water (fecal-oral route).
Incubation period: 7–14 days (range 3–60 days).

SYMPTOMS
Week 1: Gradually rising fever (stepwise pattern), headache, malaise, dry cough
Week 2: High fever (39–40°C), relative bradycardia (slow pulse despite high fever),
         abdominal discomfort, constipation or diarrhea, rose spots (on trunk)
Week 3: Complications may develop — intestinal perforation, hemorrhage, peritonitis

DIAGNOSIS
- Blood culture: gold standard (positive in 80% of cases in first week)
- Widal test: ≥1:160 for O antigen or ≥1:80 for H antigen (supportive, not definitive)
- Typhidot: rapid IgM/IgG test
- CBC: leukopenia (low WBC), anemia, elevated liver enzymes

TREATMENT
Adults (first-line):
- Azithromycin: 1g on day 1, then 500mg daily for 6 days
- Ciprofloxacin: 500mg twice daily for 10–14 days (check local resistance patterns)
- Ceftriaxone: 2g IV daily for 10–14 days (for severe cases)

PREVENTION
- Safe drinking water (boil or treat water)
- Proper sewage disposal and sanitation
- Hand washing with soap
- Typhoid vaccine (oral or injectable — recommended for travelers to endemic areas)

SECTION 4: PNEUMONIA
========================================

OVERVIEW
Pneumonia is an infection of the lung parenchyma causing consolidation.
Classified as: Community-Acquired (CAP), Hospital-Acquired (HAP), or Aspiration pneumonia.
Common pathogens: Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common), Haemophilus influenzae,
                   Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Legionella, viruses (influenza, COVID-19)

SYMPTOMS
- Fever (>38°C), chills
- Productive cough (yellow, green, or rust-colored sputum)
- Pleuritic chest pain (sharp, worsens with breathing)
- Shortness of breath (dyspnea)
- Fatigue and myalgia
- Confusion in elderly patients

DIAGNOSIS
- Chest X-ray: consolidation, infiltrates (required for diagnosis)
- CBC: elevated WBC with neutrophilia (bacterial), lymphocytosis (viral)
- Sputum culture: identifies pathogen and sensitivities
- Blood culture: bacteremia present in ~25% of CAP cases
- Procalcitonin: elevated in bacterial pneumonia

SEVERITY ASSESSMENT (CURB-65 Score)
1 point each for:
- Confusion
- Urea >7 mmol/L
- Respiratory rate ≥30/min
- Blood pressure <90/60 mmHg
- Age ≥65

Score 0–1: Home treatment
Score 2: Hospital admission
Score 3–5: ICU consideration

TREATMENT
Mild CAP (outpatient):
- Amoxicillin: 500mg three times daily for 5–7 days
- Azithromycin: 500mg day 1, then 250mg for 4 days (atypical coverage)
- Doxycycline: 100mg twice daily for 5–7 days

Moderate-Severe CAP (inpatient):
- Beta-lactam + macrolide: IV Amoxicillin-clavulanate + Azithromycin
- Or: IV Ceftriaxone 1–2g daily + Azithromycin
- Respiratory fluoroquinolone (e.g., Levofloxacin) monotherapy alternative

Supportive care:
- Oxygen therapy (target SpO2 ≥ 94%)
- IV fluids if dehydrated
- Bronchodilators if wheezing
- Chest physiotherapy

PREVENTION
- Pneumococcal vaccine (PCV13, PPSV23)
- Influenza vaccine annually
- Smoking cessation
- Hand hygiene
